Stage 09 / What Didn't Make It Every cut made the site more itself.

The Editing Process

The site was defined as much
by what was removed as what stayed.

Design is editing. This documents every feature, page, section, and idea that was explored, built, or seriously considered — and the specific reason it was cut. Nothing was removed carelessly.

Evolution by Version

What changed between
each version of the site.

V01

Structure-only — everything included

The first version treated completeness as a goal. Every possible feature was roughed in: onboarding modal, social links, blog, testimonials, portfolio galleries, direct messaging. The page was long, noisy, and unfocused. The site was trying to be everything.

Onboarding modal Blog Testimonials section Portfolio galleries Social media links Direct messaging Search bar
V02

Typography and tokens added — still too dense

The design system came in — Cormorant Garamond, Inter, the colour tokens. The page looked better but the content problem remained. The hero had three paragraphs. The how-it-works section had six steps. The membership area had a featured/highlighted "recommended" tier. Still too much.

Hero bullet points Background grid pattern Supporting hero image "Recommended" tier highlight 6-step how-it-works
V03

Subtraction pass — the rule: if it can go, it goes

Every element was questioned: does this help the user understand what Coterie is, and what to do next? If not, it was removed. The hero became two lines of text and one CTA. The apply form went from 12 fields to 4. The membership cards became three equal, unfilled cards. The footer lost everything except links and copyright.

8 form fields Footer newsletter Footer social icons Secondary nav Decorative rule patterns
Final

Only what earned its place

Every element on the final site was actively chosen. Nothing is there because it was the default. No element is there because "most sites have this." Each one has a reason.

Features Considered & Rejected

Ideas that were seriously explored
and deliberately cut.

✕

Public member profiles

Searchable portfolio pages visible without logging in

Why it was cut Making members publicly searchable removes the core reason to pay for membership. The value of Coterie is that you're not on LinkedIn — you're not publicly indexed. Visibility should be controlled, private, and earned. Public profiles would have made Coterie a free directory.
Research stage
✕

Testimonials section

Quotes from members or brands about their experience

Why it was cut Testimonials signal insecurity. Reference brands like The Row and Lemaire never show customer quotes on their sites. Coterie doesn't need social proof — if the network is genuinely valuable, the word travels privately. Putting testimonials on the homepage would undermine the brand position.
V01 cut
✕

Blog / editorial section

Industry articles, trend reports, interview series

Why it was cut A blog would require constant upkeep and positions Coterie as a media outlet rather than a private network. It would also dilute the clarity of the site's purpose. The site has one job: get creatives and brands to apply. Editorial content is a distraction from that.
V01 cut
✕

Social media links in footer

Instagram, LinkedIn, and Twitter/X icons in the footer nav

Why it was cut A private network that promotes its social media presence contradicts itself. Social media is public by nature. The footer linking to Instagram would undermine the "private, by application only" positioning on every page. What's private stays private.
V02 cut
✕

Highlighted "recommended" tier

One membership card visually elevated — filled background, "Most Popular" badge

Why it was cut Pushing users toward a specific tier feels like upselling, which is the wrong tone for Coterie. The network trusts users to know their own situation. Three equal cards — no fills, no pressure — lets the user choose without being steered. The decision is theirs.
V02 cut
✕

Onboarding modal / welcome overlay

A pop-up on first visit explaining what Coterie is

Why it was cut A modal on arrival signals that the page can't communicate its purpose on its own. If the homepage hero needs a pop-up to clarify itself, the hero isn't working. The modal was removed and the homepage copy was sharpened instead until it stood alone.
V01 cut
✕

Background grid / texture pattern

A decorative CSS grid overlay on the hero section

Why it was cut The pattern added visual complexity without adding meaning. The reference brands (The Row, Lemaire, Toteme) use pure white or off-white backgrounds with no texture. The decorative grid made the hero feel like a startup template rather than a luxury brand. Cut immediately in V02.
V02 cut
✕

Newsletter sign-up in footer

Email capture with "Stay in the know" or similar prompt

Why it was cut Newsletter sign-ups are a mass-market acquisition tool. They imply that Coterie wants as many subscribers as possible. The site's entire logic is the opposite — selective access, application-only, quality over quantity. A newsletter form in the footer was tonally incompatible.
V03 cut

Pages That Were Planned But Cut

Not every page made it
to the final site map.

Page
What it was meant to do
Why it was cut
/members
A browse page — filter creatives by discipline, location, availability
Public member browsing removes the gated nature of the network. Access to member profiles is an inside-the-platform feature, not a marketing page.
/about
The founding story, team, and mission statement
An "About" page makes Coterie talk about itself. The site is for users, not for the brand. The homepage already positions what Coterie is — an About page would repeat it in a longer, more self-indulgent way.
/journal
Editorial content — industry stories, member spotlights, trend pieces
Maintaining editorial requires resource. More critically, it dilutes the site's single purpose. Every page should make a user more likely to apply — a journal does not.
/events
A public listing of Coterie-hosted events and showrooms
Events are a member-only benefit. Listing them publicly reduces the incentive to apply and join. Events stayed as a feature mention, not a public page.
/login
A public-facing login page for existing members
The marketing site and the member platform are separate products. Login belongs inside the platform, not the marketing site. The site's only job is conversion — once inside, members are in a different environment.

All-dark homepage

An entirely black homepage — dark background, white text throughout. Tested as a "luxury" direction. Rejected because it felt heavy and exclusionary rather than quiet and selective. The reference brands (The Row, Lemaire) all use white. Dark was reserved for the nav and footer where it provides contrast, not for the full page.

All-Cormorant, italic-heavy copy

A direction using Cormorant Garamond for body text as well as headings, with italic copy throughout. Felt overwrought — too many serifs made the page feel like a wedding invitation. The italic headline copy ("Where fashion's finest come together") read as aspirational waffle. Replaced with plain, direct language set in Inter for body.

Discipline cards with member counts

An early homepage concept showing each creative discipline as a card with a live member count. Rejected on two grounds: it made membership numbers public (reducing scarcity appeal), and the card grid felt like a marketplace or job board. The final disciplines section uses a simple list — no counts, no cards.

Filled button CTAs

Solid black filled buttons with white uppercase text — the standard CTA approach. Tested early and rejected quickly. Filled buttons feel mass-market and high-pressure, visually competing with content. The final approach uses a single underlined text link (border-bottom only) as the CTA — less dominant, but more considered.
