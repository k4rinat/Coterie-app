Stage 07 / UX Decisions Friction, hierarchy, and mobile — thinking through the user experience.

UX Decisions

Every decision was made for a reason.

Area
What was decided
Why
Single CTA
Every page has one action: Apply. Nothing else competes.
Multiple CTAs cause decision paralysis. One action, one goal, higher conversion.
No pricing on homepage
Pricing is on its own page. The homepage never mentions cost.
Introducing price too early filters by budget before the value is established. Value first, price later.
Application form: 4 fields
Name, email, discipline, portfolio link. That's all we ask.
Original form had 12 fields. Each additional field increases drop-off. 4 fields is the minimum to qualify an applicant.
No social proof on homepage
No testimonials, no logos of brands, no member counts.
Exclusivity is undermined by social proof. A private network doesn't advertise who's in it. The restraint is the signal.
Separate brand journey
For Brands is a dedicated page, not a tab on the homepage.
Brands and creatives have different needs and different questions. Mixing them on one page creates a confused experience for both.
FAQ page
All real objections addressed directly. No sales language.
People apply when uncertainty is removed. The FAQ page exists to remove every possible reason not to apply.
Disciplines section
Every role on the platform listed by name on the homepage.
Seeing your exact job title on a page is a stronger signal than any headline. It says: this was built for people like you specifically.

Friction Audit

Every point of friction identified and removed.

01

Too many form fields

The first version of the apply form had 12 fields including a cover letter, LinkedIn URL, agency experience, and availability. Most users abandoned it.

→ Reduced to 4 fields

Name, email, primary discipline, portfolio link. Enough to assess an application. Everything else can be covered at interview stage.

02

Unclear what happens after applying

The original form had no confirmation message, no timeline, no next steps. Submitting felt like sending into a void.

→ Success state with timeline

After submitting: confirmation message, a 5–7 day review window, and what to expect next. Reduces anxiety, increases trust.

03

No way back after reading pricing

The pricing page had no CTA at the bottom. Users would read the pricing, then had nowhere to go — no obvious next step.

→ Apply CTA at bottom of every page

Every page ends with an Apply CTA. No page is a dead end. The journey always continues.

04

Mobile navigation broken

The desktop nav didn't adapt at smaller screen sizes. The wordmark and all links collided below 768px.

→ Responsive hamburger menu

Below 768px: wordmark + hamburger icon. On open: full-width vertical nav. Clean, no collisions, works at all sizes.

Visual Hierarchy

What the eye should see first. Second. Third.

Level 1 — Primary attention

Display heading (Cormorant 72px)
Apply CTA

Largest text on the page. The eye lands here first. Every page has exactly one Level 1 element.

Level 2 — Section headings

Section heading (Cormorant 36px)
Pillar title (Cormorant 22px)
Tier card name (Cormorant 28px)

Secondary serif headings. Same typeface as Level 1, smaller scale. Creates continuity without competition.

Level 3 — Body and supporting content

Body copy (Inter Light 16px)
Feature list items
Nav links
Descriptions

All in Inter 300 at various sizes. Never competes with the serif headings.

Level 4 — Labels and metadata

Stage labels (Inter 500, 9px, spaced)
Tier labels
Slugs
Copyright

All-caps, tracked, 9px. The quietest elements. Provide context without demanding attention.

Hamburger at 768px

Below 768px, the full nav collapses to a wordmark and a hamburger icon. Tapping it opens a full-width vertical menu overlay. The Apply CTA is the last item, most prominent.

Pillars stack vertically

The three-column Pillars section becomes a vertical list on mobile. Each pillar gets its own row with a bottom border. The layout adapts but the content doesn't change.

Membership cards stack

Side-by-side tier cards stack vertically on mobile. Each card is still full width with the same border-only style. No visual hierarchy between them changes.

Apply form: full width

The four apply fields are each full width on mobile. The submit CTA spans the full width. Minimum tap target sizes respected throughout.
