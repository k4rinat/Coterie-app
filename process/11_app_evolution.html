01 — What the App Is

A native mobile app.
Completely separate from the website.

The Coterie website is a Next.js marketing site. The Coterie app is a separate product — a React Native / Expo mobile application for iOS and Android. The website converts visitors into applicants. The app serves accepted members. It is the platform they live in after being accepted. Different screens, different navigation, different design philosophy — features that only make sense on a phone.

06 — What Was Removed

Features that were planned
and cut before launch.

Every feature below had a reason to exist. Each was cut for an equally specific reason. The app is better for their absence.

Navigation
Sidebar / Hamburger Menu
First navigation model. Borrowed from web. Replaced with bottom tab bar because thumb reach makes hamburger menus biomechanically wrong on mobile. Two-tap navigation for primary screens is never acceptable.
Screen
Discover / Browse Tab
A screen for exploring members and brands publicly. Cut because Coterie is not a browsing platform — it is a mediated one. Open discovery contradicts the gatekeeping model. Introductions are facilitated, not self-initiated browsing.
Screen
Standalone Notifications Tab
Notifications as a primary tab implied that the app was notification-driven — a content feed model. Coterie is not. Notifications surface through push and through the Home activity feed. Giving them their own tab inflated the navigation without justification.
Screen
Standalone Settings Tab
Settings are infrequent actions — language, theme, notifications, sign out. Elevating these to a primary tab wastes cognitive real estate. Folded into Profile under progressive disclosure: they're there when you need them, invisible when you don't.
Feature
Public Member Directory
A searchable directory of all members. Cut because it undermined the exclusivity model — members should be findable to each other only through Coterie's introductions process, not through a public search. Visibility is mediated.
Feature
Generic Help Chatbot
The first version of Éloi was a generic FAQ chatbot with platform-only answers. Cut and rebuilt as a named concierge with a luxury fashion knowledge base. A help chatbot communicates utility; Éloi communicates identity.

07 — Éloi

The AI concierge.
12 languages. One identity.

Éloi is not a support bot. It is a named character — Coterie's concierge — with a defined voice, a luxury fashion knowledge base, and the ability to respond in the language of the member asking. The name comes from the French given name, evoking the refinement of the Maison culture Coterie inhabits.

Sample Conversation

What's the difference between a maison and an atelier?
Éloi
A maison — French for "house" — refers to a luxury fashion brand with heritage and identity. An atelier is the workshop where garments are made by hand. A maison may have one or several ateliers. Chanel's haute couture atelier on Rue Cambon is distinct from Chanel as a maison, though inseparable from its reputation.

09 — Splash Screen

Three seconds.
Every moment intentional.

The splash screen is not a loading spinner with branding on it. It is a composed animation with a specific duration, a specific sequence, and a specific reason for each timing value. The total display time is 3 seconds, set deliberately by the developer. The timer doesn't start until the app has confirmed your login state — so the 3 seconds is guaranteed screen time, never cut short.

Why 3 seconds exactly?
The animation finishes at roughly 2.1 seconds. The remaining ~900ms is deliberate hold time — long enough to feel unhurried, short enough to not feel slow. The splash should feel like a breath, not a wait. Three seconds was the number that felt right in testing: under two felt rushed; over three felt impatient.
Why does the tagline slide up?
An 8px upward drift on the tagline gives it a sense of arrival — it doesn't just appear, it surfaces. The movement is small enough that most people won't consciously register it, but it makes the entrance feel more alive than a plain opacity fade would.

10 — Visual Design Language

Why the app looks the way it does.
Every decision had a reason.

The visual language of the Coterie app is not decorative — it is communicative. Dark mode, rounded corners, frosted glass, restrained typography, symbolic badges — each of these choices says something specific about the kind of platform Coterie is, and the kind of people it serves.

Design Decision
Theme Follows
Time of Day
The app has no fixed default theme. On every launch it reads the device's local time and sets the mode automatically: dark from 6pm to 6am, light from 6am to 6pm. A background interval rechecks every 60 seconds, so the theme can shift live while the app is open. Manually toggling in Profile disables auto-switching for that session only — the next launch reads the clock again.
Light — 6:00 → 18:00
Dark — 18:00 → 6:00
00:0006:00 ☀︎12:0018:00 ☽00:00
Why 6pm: Evening is when the app is most likely to be used in low-light — after studio hours, at events, at dinner. Dark mode serves the context the member is actually in.
Manual override: Toggling in Profile calls setManualOverride(true) and stops the interval for that session. Next launch, the clock decides again.
Design Decision
Rounded
Everything
The phone frame itself has extreme rounded corners (36px border-radius). Cards have softer radius. Input fields and buttons follow suit. This creates what feels like a unified, cohesive shell — every component feels like it belongs to the same object.
Rounded corners read as safe, approachable, and premium. Sharp corners read as utilitarian or aggressive. In the context of luxury fashion — where product edges, stitching, and silhouette are obsessed over — the softness of the interface signals care and craft. iOS design conventions reinforce this: Apple's own design language uses extreme rounding as a mark of quality.
Sharp — feels aggressive, cheap
Slight — functional, neutral
Rounded — considered, premium
Design Decision
Frosted Glass
Tab Bar
The bottom tab bar uses a frosted glass effect on iOS. The content behind the bar is visible but blurred, giving it a sense of depth and material quality rather than a flat opaque strip.
This effect was chosen for three specific reasons: (1) It signals iOS nativeness — frosted glass is a hallmark of Apple's design since iOS 7, and members using Coterie are likely iPhone users who associate this blur with premium, trusted apps. (2) It creates visual continuity — the content doesn't abruptly end at a hard line; it dissolves into the navigation. (3) It communicates that the navigation is a surface floating above the content, not a static bar bolted to the bottom — a subtle but perceptible difference in depth.

Design Decision
Two Typefaces.
One Contrast.
The app uses exactly two typefaces, shared with the website: Cormorant Garamond (serif) and Inter (sans-serif). The contrast between them is intentional and consistent throughout.

The entire app uses no accent colour. No blue, no green, no gold. The palette is near-black, near-white, and graduated greys. This is a deliberate statement: Coterie does not need a colour to announce itself. The restraint is the brand.

11 — Haptic Feedback

Why every tap in Coterie
has a physical response.

Haptic feedback was added in V2 and applied across every meaningful interaction in the app. This was not a decorative feature. It was a deliberate UX decision about what kind of app Coterie should feel like in your hand.

The Argument For It
Touch is the medium. Make it felt.
A mobile app lives in your hand. Unlike a website — where interactions are mediated by a mouse and a screen — a phone responds to the pressure of your thumb. Haptics close the loop: you press, something happens, and you feel it. Without haptics, an app can feel like it's only partially responding to you. With them, every action feels confirmed, real, and intentional.
The Luxury Connection
Physical quality signals premium.
Luxury physical objects are defined by their tactile quality — the click of a Rolex crown, the weight of a Hermès clasp, the resistance of a Saint Laurent zipper. Haptic feedback is the digital equivalent. It adds weight and materiality to interactions that would otherwise feel weightless. For an app serving fashion professionals, this sensory detail is not trivial — it is the digital version of the quality they work with every day.

12 — Face ID & Biometric Login

One look, or a button.
The member chooses.

Biometric authentication — Face ID on iPhone, fingerprint on Android — is an optional convenience layer added on top of the standard login. The traditional email and password login remains fully available. Face ID is not a replacement; it is an enhancement for members who want a faster, frictionless re-entry after their first login.

⌥
Optional Feature
Face ID is a convenience upgrade, not a gate.
The standard login button — email and password — is always present and always works. Face ID appears as an additional option on devices that support it. A member who prefers to type their password can do so. A member who wants to look at their phone and be inside in one second can do that instead. Neither path is wrong.
Why offer it — Convenience
Zero friction for returning members.
After the first login, a member who enables Face ID never has to type their password again. For a platform they open daily to check roles and events, removing that one step compounds into a meaningfully smoother experience over time.
Why offer it — Privacy
An extra lock for sensitive content.
Coterie holds private job opportunities, personal introductions, and profile data. Members who care about this — for instance, those checking the app in public or shared spaces — can choose biometric as a second layer of security over and above their password.
Why offer it — Brand
The choice itself feels considered.
Offering Face ID without forcing it communicates respect for the member's preference. Luxury is not about imposing an experience — it is about giving people an excellent option and letting them take it. The standard login is perfectly dignified. Face ID is an upgrade for those who want it.

13 — The Small Details

The things you notice
without knowing why.

The most important design details in Coterie are the ones that don't announce themselves. They don't have feature names or screens. They are the tiny choices that accumulate into the feeling of a considered, premium product. Each one had a reason.

Detail
Time-based greeting
The Home screen opens with "Good morning.", "Good afternoon.", or "Good evening." — determined by the device's local time. Not the user's name. Not "Welcome back." Just the time of day, in Cormorant Garamond, with a full stop.
This makes the app feel alive — responsive to the real world rather than frozen in a permanent "Welcome" state. It also sets the tone for each session: morning has different energy than evening. The greeting acknowledges that.
Detail
Daily fashion quote
A rotating array of quotes from figures like Giorgio Armani, Coco Chanel, and Alexander McQueen — displayed in Cormorant Garamond italic, with a left border rule, and the attribution below. The quote changes daily.
This is the app's emotional peak moment (Peak-End Rule). A member who opens Coterie and reads a resonant Armani sentence in a typeface this beautiful has had an experience — not just seen a dashboard. It justifies the daily open.
Detail
Feather icon set
The tab bar icons use Feather Icons — a minimal, open-source icon set with stroke-based geometry at consistent weight. No filled icons. No bevels. No colour. Just clean 1.5px strokes.
Filled icons feel like Fisher-Price UI. Stroke icons feel like technical drawings — precise, deliberate, adult. They also scale without visual noise, which matters in a small tab bar at multiple display densities.
Detail
Dynamic Island / notch integration
The phone frame renders a pill-shaped notch that mirrors iPhone's Dynamic Island. Screen content begins below it. The phone chrome (rounded device edges, notch, tab bar) is built into every screen mockup in this documentation.
Designing with the notch in mind — not as an obstacle to route around, but as a frame element — means the app looks intentional on modern iPhones rather than an afterthought ported to a new form factor.
Detail
Symbolic badge characters
Earned badges on the Profile screen use Unicode symbols: ✦ ◈ ◇ ◉ ▶ ⊕ ▪ ●. No custom graphics. No image assets. Pure character encoding — which means they render at any size, on any device, in any language, with zero loading time.
Geometric Unicode symbols have an art-historical quality — they reference technical drawing, astronomical notation, and mathematical symbols. They feel earned rather than gamified. A member should feel like they received a mark of distinction, not a sticker.
Detail
Calendar integration
RSVPing to an event in Coterie adds it directly to the member's device calendar. The event appears with Coterie's name, the venue, and the time — the same way any other calendar entry does.
The alternative — only storing the RSVP inside the app — means the member has to check the app to know when the event is. Putting it in their calendar means Coterie becomes part of their actual life, not a separate place to check.
Detail
Profile photo from camera roll
Members can set a profile photo directly from their camera roll. The avatar is circular. On the Profile screen it sits next to the member's name and discipline — a small but significant human detail in an otherwise typographic interface.
In a platform of vetted creatives, who you are visually matters. A circular avatar is a social convention that communicates "this is a person" without requiring a portfolio view. The member chose this image — it is their professional face on the platform.
Detail
Full stop at end of greeting
"Good morning." — the period is intentional. Not "Good morning!" (too eager) or "Good morning" without punctuation (grammatically incomplete). The full stop is calm, unhurried, and adult. It matches the rest of the copy voice exactly.
This is one single character. It is also the clearest example of how seriously the copy and design are aligned in Coterie. The punctuation communicates personality. The period says: we are not shouting at you. We are simply acknowledging the time of day.

Why iOS-style toggles

The pill shape
Extreme rounding signals that this element is interactive — it wants to be pressed. Consistent with every other rounded element in the app.
The thumb position
Off
→
On
Left = off. Right = on. A universal convention the member already knows from their phone's own settings. No learning required.
The colour logic
No green. No blue. On dark mode: white track = on, dark grey = off. On light mode: near-black track = on, light grey = off. The toggle always matches the app's palette — it never introduces a new colour.
Settings live inside Profile. They're available whenever a member needs them — but they're never in the way. Progressive disclosure: visible on demand, invisible otherwise.

15 — The Complete Picture

All the decisions,
in one place.

Every choice in the Coterie app — visual, interactive, typographic, tactile — points in the same direction. Here is the full stack of decisions, layered from the most abstract to the most specific.

Layer
Decision
Why
01
Native mobile app (React Native / Expo)
Access to device hardware: Face ID, haptics, camera, calendar. Web apps can't do this natively.
02
Theme auto-switches by time of day
Dark 6pm–6am, light 6am–6pm using the device's local clock. Rechecks every 60 seconds. Manual toggle overrides for the session only.
03
No accent colour
Restraint is the brand. Hierarchy through tone, not colour.
04
Cormorant Garamond + Inter
Editorial serif for headings; functional sans for everything else. One tension, one resolution.
05
Rounded corners throughout
Craft and care. Every component feels like it belongs to the same premium object.
06
Frosted glass tab bar
Depth, material quality, iOS nativeness. The nav floats above content rather than blocking it.
07
Bottom tab bar, 5 items (Hick + Fitts)
Thumb zone navigation. One tap to any primary screen. Cognitive load minimised.
08
Face ID login
Zero friction. Privacy protection. Access is personal — it should feel personal.
09
Haptic feedback
Touch is the medium. Interactions are felt, not just seen. Tactile quality = perceived premium quality.
10
Time-based greeting + daily quote
Peak-End Rule. Emotional peak on every open. Makes the app worth returning to daily.
11
Éloi in 12 languages
Luxury fashion is global. The concierge should speak your language without requiring a settings change.
12
Unicode badge symbols
Marks of distinction, not gamification stickers. No image assets. Universal rendering.
13
Native calendar integration
Coterie becomes part of the member's actual life, not a separate silo to remember to check.
14
Full stop after "Good morning."
Copy and design are the same discipline. One character communicates the entire tone of the platform.
